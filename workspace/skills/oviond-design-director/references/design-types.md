# Design Types

Use the relevant section for the requested deliverable.

## Social post

Goal: stop the scroll, communicate one idea, stay brand-consistent.

Use when:
- educational posts
- feature highlights
- positioning posts
- stat cards
- event promos

Rules:
- one idea per asset
- limit supporting elements
- prioritize headline readability
- design for mobile-first viewing

## Paid social ad

Goal: get attention and communicate value fast enough to earn the click.

Rules:
- lead with the strongest value prop or pain point
- use higher contrast than organic social
- simplify backgrounds
- keep CTA area clean
- avoid visual cleverness that delays understanding

## Display ad

Goal: instant message recognition in constrained space.

Rules:
- fewer elements than social
- stronger logo/brand anchoring
- prioritize clarity at small sizes
- avoid fine detail that disappears

## Landing-page hero visual

Goal: support trust and comprehension above the fold.

Rules:
- complement the headline, do not compete with it
- support page layout and whitespace needs
- keep it scalable for responsive layouts
- favor product-adjacent visuals, conceptual clarity, or agency outcomes

## Blog featured image

Goal: make content look polished and relevant.

Rules:
- simpler than an ad
- concept should map cleanly to article theme
- avoid sensational thumbnail energy
- keep styles reusable across a content series

## Email banner / lifecycle asset

Goal: reinforce message without adding friction.

Rules:
- keep dimensions and composition simple
- low visual noise
- strong readability
- should survive dark/light email client environments when adapted

## Webinar / event promo

Goal: drive registration and quickly explain who/what/when.

Rules:
- event title must dominate
- supporting details should have obvious secondary hierarchy
- speaker elements should not overcrowd the layout
- leave room for date/time and CTA placement

## Comparison / feature graphic

Goal: make differences obvious.

Rules:
- use structured layout
- keep categories consistent
- avoid overloaded matrixes unless truly needed
- clarity beats completeness

## Infographic / stat card

Goal: communicate one memorable number or insight.

Rules:
- one hero stat
- one short supporting line max
- obvious hierarchy
- use contrast and spacing to make the number do the work
