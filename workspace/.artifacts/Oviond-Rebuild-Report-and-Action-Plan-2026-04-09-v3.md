## Executive summary

This version upgrades the earlier rebuild report by combining two separate evidence streams that point to the same conclusion.

The first evidence stream is the full Gleap support history and the higher-signal product subset already analysed. The second is the buyer-era internal product review you shared, which is unusually blunt and therefore extremely valuable.

Taken together, they say the same thing.

Oviond's core problem is not market size. It is not top-of-funnel demand. It is not a lack of features in the abstract. The real issue is product credibility.

The product is being experienced as unpolished, structurally unreliable, weak in transformation and reporting depth, shaky in connectors and data trust, shallow in automation, and overstated in its AI positioning. That combination does three kinds of damage at once:

- it increases support burden
- it weakens activation and retention
- it kills buyer and operator confidence during serious evaluation

The buyer review matters because it confirms that this is not just noisy support friction from weaker users. A serious evaluator reached much the same conclusion that the support history implies: Oviond currently feels too immature for professional agency reporting teams.

That is painful, but it is clean signal.

The rebuild therefore needs to be a trust, capability, and credibility rebuild. It should not be treated as a cosmetic refresh.

## The real strategic diagnosis now

### 1. The deal did not tank because the buyer wanted too much

The deal appears to have tanked because product quality did not support the value story. That is a very different problem from feature envy.

### 2. Oviond currently has a credibility gap, not just a capability gap

A capability gap says "we need more features." A credibility gap says "the market does not believe us when we describe what the product is." The buyer review makes clear that Oviond is currently suffering from both.

### 3. The rebuild must close three gaps at once

- the capability gap, by making the product actually able to do serious agency reporting work
- the reliability gap, by making outputs, connectors, and delivery trustworthy
- the positioning gap, by only claiming what is visibly and repeatably true

### 4. The rebuild goal should not be feature parity with giant BI tools

That would be stupid. Oviond should not try to become Power BI or Tableau.

The better target is this:

**A dependable, agency-grade reporting operating system that is easier than enterprise BI, more trustworthy than lightweight reporting toys, and stronger on white-label delivery, reporting workflows, and client-ready output.**

Source: MEMORY.md#L23-L31; Oviond – Internal Product Review, section 2.

## Rebuild principles, the rules we should not break

### Principle 1. No claim before capability

If the product does not visibly do the thing, marketing and sales should not imply that it does.

### Principle 2. Trust beats novelty

A stable connector, a correct metric, and a reliable scheduled report matter more than an extra surface feature.

### Principle 3. Reporting depth matters in paid media and social

The buyer review is explicit that PPC and social teams will not tolerate shallow, flaky reporting. That means those areas cannot be hand-waved.

### Principle 4. Keep simplicity, remove shallowness

The product should stay easy. It should just stop being weak.

### Principle 5. White-label delivery is core product, not add-on admin

White-label, custom domains, branded delivery, and client-ready outputs are part of the primary value proposition.

### Principle 6. AI must be honest, useful, and clearly secondary to reporting truth

No ghost AI. No empty positioning. Ship AI only where it is visibly real and actually helpful.

### Principle 7. Every major reporting surface must agree

Editor, shared report, PDF, automation, and email output must resolve the same data correctly.

Source: MEMORY.md#L23-L31; MEMORY.md#L46-L53; Oviond – Internal Product Review, sections 2, 3, 5, and 6.

## What must be preserved from the current product

This is important. The rebuild should not destroy the few things the product clearly does well.

- simple setup
- basic top-level reporting speed
- public share links without login
- easy scheduled report delivery
- white-label value proposition
- lower-friction agency usability versus heavyweight BI tools

The goal is not to replace Oviond's simplicity with enterprise bloat. The goal is to make simplicity trustworthy.

# Expanded rebuild action plan

## P0. Mandatory truth and scope reset

### 1. Reset positioning and sales language immediately
**Why**  
The buyer review shows a severe expectation mismatch between what is claimed and what is actually in the product. That mismatch destroys diligence confidence fast.

**Action notes**
- Remove or soften claims that overstate AI, automation depth, cross-channel robustness, or all-in-one maturity until shipped reality supports them.
- Reframe Oviond around agency reporting simplicity, branded delivery, and dependable recurring reporting.
- Build product marketing around what can be demoed cleanly without caveats.
- Create an internal claims matrix: claim, proof, demo path, known caveats, owner.

Source: Oviond – Internal Product Review, sections 2 and 5.

### 2. Define the actual target product, agency-grade reporting OS
**Why**  
Without a clear target, the rebuild will sprawl.

**Action notes**
- Position the rebuild as an agency-grade reporting operating system.
- Core pillars: connector trust, transformation depth, client-ready delivery, permissions, automation, and white-labeling.
- Explicitly do not chase generic BI platform parity.
- Use paid media, social, white-label reporting, and recurring delivery as the primary use-case lens.

Source: MEMORY.md#L23-L31.

### 3. Freeze vanity feature expansion until the reporting core is credible
**Why**  
Shipping more shallow features before fixing the foundation will compound support debt.

**Action notes**
- Put a governance gate on new non-core features.
- Require that every major new feature survive the questions: does it improve trust, depth, delivery, or retention?
- Shift roadmap weight to reliability and serious-use reporting depth first.

## P1. Reporting-core rebuild, the heart of the product

### 4. Build a real transformation layer
**Why**  
This is one of the clearest buyer-review failures and one of the most important capability gaps in the whole product.

**Action notes**
- Support calculated fields and custom metrics.
- Support derived KPIs and formula composition.
- Support transformation logic cleanly across connectors.
- Add preview, validation, and error messaging.
- Make transformation capabilities available consistently in builder, shared output, and automation output.

Source: Oviond – Internal Product Review, section 4.

### 5. Support cross-source metric combinations safely
**Why**  
Serious agency reporting depends on combining costs, conversions, leads, and revenue across sources.

**Action notes**
- Define allowed cross-source operations and grain rules.
- Prevent mathematically invalid joins or misleading mixed-grain calculations.
- Add explicit source mapping and formula traceability.
- Provide clear warnings when a formula could produce misleading output.

Source: Oviond – Internal Product Review, section 4; earlier Gleap calculation issues.

### 6. Add proper segmentation and filtering, including conversion-action filtering
**Why**  
The buyer review explicitly calls out the inability to filter by conversion action. That is fatal for serious PPC workflows.

**Action notes**
- Support conversion-action and event-level filtering where connector APIs allow it.
- Support typed filter operators by metric or dimension type.
- Add save-time validation for unsupported filters.
- Preserve filters consistently across builder and output surfaces.

Source: Oviond – Internal Product Review, section 4.

### 7. Fix date-dimension and time-grain integrity across paid media reporting
**Why**  
The buyer review calls out a campaign-level date-dimension bug. That is not cosmetic. It corrupts analysis.

**Action notes**
- Standardise time grain across queries.
- Ensure campaign-level and asset-level views resolve dates correctly.
- Add regression tests for daily, weekly, monthly, and custom-date slices.
- Make date-range logic identical in editor, shared, PDF, and automation contexts.

Source: Oviond – Internal Product Review, section 4.

### 8. Rebuild the query engine and schema model
**Why**  
A weak schema layer creates downstream fragility everywhere, filters, calculations, widgets, exports, and automations.

**Action notes**
- Build a typed schema model for metrics, dimensions, and grains.
- Formalise connector metadata rather than relying on loose UI assumptions.
- Add query linting and schema-aware builder constraints.
- Expose query debugging for support and advanced users.

Source: Oviond – Internal Product Review, section 4; MEMORY.md#L46-L53.

### 9. Make multi-source reporting trustworthy instead of brittle
**Why**  
The buyer review says multi-source data on one page often breaks or duplicates values. That kills cross-channel credibility.

**Action notes**
- Define explicit multi-source blending rules.
- Block unsupported blends rather than producing misleading outputs.
- Add blended-query diagnostics and previews.
- Create fixture-based tests for common agency reporting combinations.

Source: Oviond – Internal Product Review, section 4.

## P2. Connector and data-trust platform

### 10. Rebuild connector infrastructure as a first-class platform service
**Why**  
Gleap and the buyer review both point to connectors as a major trust problem.

**Action notes**
- One central connection service.
- Standardised OAuth, token refresh, scope validation, and account discovery.
- Health states: connected, degraded, expired, permission issue, upstream issue.
- Clear reconnect flows and diagnostics.
- Last successful sync and last failed sync visible in-product.

Source: MEMORY.md#L46-L53; Oviond – Internal Product Review, section 4.

### 11. Harden Meta, Facebook, and Instagram connections specifically
**Why**  
The buyer review is explicit that Facebook connections frequently fail and page or profile selection is unreliable. Gleap also repeatedly surfaced Meta-side issues.

**Action notes**
- Rework account, page, and profile selection UX.
- Add permission preflight before save.
- Add entitlement troubleshooting for Business Manager edge cases.
- Add exact account identity confirmation so users know what they connected.
- Add sync diagnostics for partial or stale social data.

Source: Oviond – Internal Product Review, section 4.

### 12. Fix LinkedIn data correctness before treating LinkedIn as saleable
**Why**  
The buyer review says multiple LinkedIn profiles show identical numbers. That is a catastrophic trust failure.

**Action notes**
- Audit LinkedIn mapping logic and identifier resolution.
- Build profile-level data-validation fixtures.
- Add customer-visible source labels and account references.
- Suspend aggressive selling of LinkedIn reporting until correctness is proven.

Source: Oviond – Internal Product Review, section 4.

### 13. Build a priority connector program, not random connector maintenance
**Why**  
The connector problem is not just bug fixing. It needs portfolio management.

**Action notes**
- Rank connectors by usage, revenue impact, churn risk, support burden, and buyer importance.
- Create one owner per critical connector.
- Add release gates for regression risk on top connectors.
- Maintain a public or internal status sheet for connector capability depth.

Source: MEMORY.md#L23-L31; MEMORY.md#L46-L53.

### 14. Expose data freshness and failure diagnostics everywhere
**Why**  
Users and support currently spend too much time guessing whether a problem is auth, data lag, bad filters, or upstream failure.

**Action notes**
- Widget-level freshness badges.
- Data-source health drawer.
- Empty-state explanations.
- Support diagnostics export.
- Provider-incident messaging.
- Comparison diagnostics for editor versus shared output.

Source: MEMORY.md#L46-L53.

## Pass-fail conditions before we call the rebuild ready

If Oviond still fails these, the rebuild is not done.

### A. Buyer-confidence conditions
- No evaluator should be able to honestly describe the product as an MVP.
- No major capability claim should feel materially overstated in a live demo.
- Paid media and social workflows must survive scrutiny from real specialists.

### B. Reporting-trust conditions
- Cross-source and derived metrics work reliably.
- Editor, shared report, PDF, and automation output match.
- Data freshness and error states are obvious.
- Multi-source reporting does not silently duplicate or corrupt outputs.

### C. Connector-trust conditions
- Top connectors are reliable, diagnosable, and well-scoped.
- Meta and LinkedIn no longer fail basic trust checks.
- Reconnect and token-expiry flows are sane.

### D. Agency-operations conditions
- White-label and custom-domain setup is self-serve and stable.
- Permissions and client sharing are predictable.
- Bulk agency workflows are available.
- Billing and limit states are clear.

### E. Quality conditions
- Rounding, formatting, and navigation bugs are not embarrassing.
- Regression coverage exists for critical workflows.
- Support signal falls materially after pilot rollout.

## Final recommendation

The right lesson from the buyer review is not "we need more features."

The right lesson is this:

**Oviond currently loses confidence faster than it earns it.**

Gleap already suggested that. The buyer review confirms it.

So the rebuild needs to be built around confidence creation:

- confidence that the connector is real
- confidence that the data is correct
- confidence that the calculation is valid
- confidence that the output will render properly
- confidence that the client will receive the right thing
- confidence that what marketing says is actually true

If we get that right, Oviond becomes much more than a cheap lightweight reporting tool. It becomes a genuinely credible agency reporting platform with a clear lane.

If we do not get that right, the rebuild will still leak trust, and the team will be back in the same strategic hole with a cleaner interface.

That would be a waste.
